Abilities

One Punch:

One Punch: When making an unarmed attack you may choose to roll a single Attack dice. If this attack hits, it is resolved at Strength 8 and Damage 2, and no Save roll can be made (with the exception of Field armour).

True Grit:

True Grit: When making an Injury roll for this fighter, roll one less Injury dice (for example, a Damage 2 weapon would roll one dice). Against attacks with Damage 1, roll two dice – the player controlling the fighter with True Grit can then choose one dice to discard before the effects of the other are resolved.

Fighter Backstory

Isaac Dent has never needed to be the loudest man in a fight. He only needs one opening.

The nickname One Punch came from the pits, where a swaggering champion twice his size spent five minutes taunting him and half a second hitting the floor. Isaac waited, timed the breath, shifted his weight, and ended it with one strike so clean the crowd went silent before it roared. He walked out without looking back. That part matters to him more than the win.

As Road Sergeant, Isaac organizes the gang under Grinnarg and alongside Hank and Tyra, but his role is distinct: he turns intent into impact. If Grinnarg decides what must happen, Isaac decides exactly where pressure lands first. Breach teams follow him because he does not waste lives on vanity pushes. Raphael trusts his target calls. Karl trusts his angles. Joy studies his pacing, and though some still call her a greenhorn, Isaac has started handing her tougher lanes.

With prospects he is brutally fair. Charly gets praised only when timing is perfect. Andy gets corrected every time courage outruns sense. Barnabas gets one warning, then no charges for a week if he freelances. Harsh, maybe. Effective, absolutely.

Isaac’s private fear is not dying-it is failing the line and leaving someone behind because he rushed a decision. So he trains, measures, and trains again. When he says “Move,” the gang moves. They know he already spent the doubt before anyone heard the order.

After actions, he walks the field slowly, replaying every decision in silence. Which corner was late. Which call was clean. Which wound was avoidable. He carries those lessons into the next run and expects everyone else to do the same. That is why newer fighters fear his critiques and crave them anyway: Isaac turns mistakes into survival.
